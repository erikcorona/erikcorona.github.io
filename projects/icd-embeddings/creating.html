Let's Create ICD-10 Embeddings!

We use a BERT-style masked language model to learn ICD-10 embeddings. The model takes a patient's claim history as a sequence of up to 512 positions, masks a subset of ICD-10 codes, and learns to predict them from surrounding clinical context.

Training in Two Passes

The first pass masks 15% of ICD-10 codes at random. This teaches the model the basic structure of clinical data: which codes tend to appear together, what procedures and specialties co-occur with which diagnoses, and how conditions relate to patient demographics.

But there's a problem. Many ICD-10 codes repeat within a patient's history, especially chronic conditions like Type 2 diabetes. If the model sees diabetes at positions 1, 4, and 9, and only position 4 is masked, the answer is trivially visible at positions 1 and 9. The model can learn a shortcut: if a code appeared before, it will appear again. That's not clinical insight. It's just pattern matching on repetition.

The second pass fixes this by masking 15% of unique ICD-10 codes, then hiding all occurrences of each selected code. If diabetes is chosen for masking, every instance of it disappears from the sequence. Now the model can't rely on repetition. It has to infer diabetes from the surrounding context: the medications, lab values, complications, and co-occurring conditions that actually signal the disease. This forces the model to learn the true relationships between codes, not just how often they repeat.
